## 📋 Employee Handbook — Key Policies

### Code of Conduct
- Treat patients, colleagues, and community members with respect
- Maintain patient confidentiality (HIPAA)
- Report safety concerns immediately
- Follow all clinical protocols without deviation
- No substances (drugs/alcohol) while on duty
- Professional appearance and demeanor required

### Attendance & Scheduling
- Shifts are scheduled in 2-week rotating blocks
- Notify management of schedule conflicts ASAP
- Unexcused absences: verbal warning → written warning → termination
- Late arrivals: crew briefing starts on time; repeated tardiness = disciplinary action

### Dress Code
- Medical uniforms: navy pants/skirt, RAM polo or flight suit
- Name badge required
- Closed-toe shoes (hiking boots OK for field ops)
- High-visibility vest/jacket when on roadways

- Field ops: appropriate tactical/technical gear per assignment

### Emergency Contact Information
- Maintain current emergency contact in HR file
- Update immediately if contact changes
- Used only in case of serious injury or emergency

### Confidentiality & PHI
- Never discuss patient information outside clinical context
- No photos/recordings of patients without explicit consent
- Medical records are confidential—no sharing
- Violations = immediate termination + potential legal action

## 🏥 Medical Operations Overview

### Units & Deployment Types

**Ambulance Units (RAMBO 1-4)**
- Basic/Advanced Life Support response
- Crew: 1-2 Paramedics + 1 EMT (typically)
- Staffed during fire season and mutual aid activations
- Primary equipment: stretcher, monitor, medications, trauma kit, O₂, airway devices

**Medical Unit (Med Unit)**
- Physician + Nurse Practitioner/PA + 1-2 support staff
- Deployed to extended incidents (wildfires, large events)
- Advanced procedures: IV placement, wound management, pain control
- Equipment: medications, surgical supplies, portable monitoring

**REMS (Remote Emergency Medical Services) Units**
- Specialized technical rope rescue + medical
- 4-person teams: 1+ Paramedic, 1+ EMT, rope specialists
- Deployment: wilderness rescue, technical terrain, multi-system operations
- Training: S-130, S-190, L-180, ICS certs required

**Warehouse (Supply/Logistics)**
- Stores controlled substances, medications, equipment
- Distribution to units, inventory tracking
- Coordinated through Operations Manager

### Role Definitions

| Role | Scope | Key Responsibilities |
|------|-------|---------------------|
| **EMT** | Basic Life Support | Patient assessment, BLS, oxygen, bandaging, transport |
| **Paramedic** | Advanced Life Support | IVs, airway mgmt, medications, field diagnosis, complex care |
| **RN** | Advanced + Nursing | IV therapy, wound management, patient education, nursing assessment |
| **NP/PA** | Physician-level | Diagnosis, treatment plans, medication orders, standing orders |
| **MD/DO** | Physician | Medical direction, complex cases, field protocols, training |

## 🏥 Sierra-Sacramento Valley (SSV) EMS Agency Treatment Protocols

RAM operates under SSV EMS Agency protocols (most common shown below). All treatment must follow these guidelines unless a physician on-scene directs otherwise.

### Scope of Practice by Level

**EMT Scope (Policy 801):**
- Patient assessment (vitals, history, physical exam)
- Oxygen therapy (via NC, mask, BVM)
- Basic airway management (recovery position, spinal precautions)
- CPR and automated external defibrillator (AED)
- Assistance with medications (epinephrine auto-injector, naloxone, aspirin, glucose)
- Transport and patient comfort

**Paramedic Scope (Policy 803):**
- All EMT skills
- IV therapy and intraosseous access (IO)
- Advanced airway management (intubation, supraglottic airways)
- Medication administration (epinephrine, amiodarone, morphine, naloxone, etc.)
- Cardiac monitoring and interpretation
- Defibrillation and synchronized cardioversion
- Transcutaneous pacing
- Needle cricothyrotomy (severe airway obstruction)
- Patient treatment per detailed protocols below

### Critical Protocols (Protocol Index)

**C-1: Pulseless Arrest (Cardiac Arrest)**
- **BLS Steps:** CPR 100-120 compressions/min, positive pressure ventilation with 100% O₂
- **Paramedic (VF/VT):**
  1. Defibrillation (biphasic 120-200J or per device)
  2. CPR × 2 min
  3. IV/IO, Epinephrine 1 mg (1:10,000) every 3-5 min
  4. Defibrillation attempt
  5. Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO (may repeat 150 mg)
- **Paramedic (Asystole/PEA):**
  1. CPR × 2 min
  2. IV/IO, Epinephrine 1 mg every 3-5 min
  3. Advanced airway when possible (intubation or supraglottic)
- **Reversible Causes to Check:** Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hyperkalemia/Hypokalemia, Hypothermia, Tamponade, Tension pneumothorax, Toxins, Thrombosis (PE/MI)
- **Termination Criteria:** No witness to arrest + no effective bystander CPR + no ROSC after full ALS + no shocks delivered

**C-5: Return of Spontaneous Circulation (Post-Arrest Care)**
- Manage airway, position of comfort, reassess vitals q 1 min
- If SBP < 90 & HR < 60: Atropine 0.5 mg IV, consider pacing, fluid bolus
- If resuscitated from VF/VT and SBP > 90: Amiodarone 150 mg slow IV (if not given)
- **ROSC Pilot Study:** Transport to closest STEMI center if ≤ 45 min transport time (exceptions: traumatic arrest, OD, drowning, age < 30)

**C-6: Tachycardia with Pulse**
- **Stable Narrow Complex:** Valsalva maneuver → Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV → Adenosine 12 mg if no response
- **Unstable or Wide Complex:** Pre-cardioversion sedation (Midazolam 0.1 mg/kg max 4 mg) → Synchronized cardioversion 100J, 200J, 300J, 360J
- **A-Fib/A-Flutter/Sinus Tach:** Treat underlying cause, do not cardiovert unless hemodynamically unstable

**C-7: Bradycardia (HR < 60)**
- **Symptomatic:** Atropine 0.5 mg IV (max 3 mg total) → Transcutaneous Pacing at 80 BPM (increase by 10 mA) with sedation
- **Pacing Sedation:** Midazolam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) OR Morphine 2-5 mg OR Fentanyl 25-50 mcg
- **Key Rule:** If symptomatic, do NOT delay pacing for IV or drugs

**C-8: Chest Pain (Suspected Cardiac)**
- ABCs, oxygen (titrate SpO₂ ≥ 94%), 12-lead ECG
- **Medications:**
  1. Aspirin 320-325 mg chewable
  2. Nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL every 5 min (hold if SBP < 100)
  3. If pain persists: Morphine 2 mg increments or Fentanyl 25 mcg increments (max 20 mg morphine equiv)
- **STEMI:** Transport to closest STEMI center (≤ 45 min), advise "STEMI ALERT," transmit 12-lead if available

**R-1: Airway Obstruction**
- High-flow O₂, position of comfort
- **Foreign Body:** Abdominal thrusts; if unconscious → CPR, check mouth
- **Paramedic:** Direct laryngoscopy, Magill forceps extraction
- **Severe Infection (Epiglottitis):** Do NOT visualize throat; consider humidified O₂, nebulized epinephrine 5 mL 1:1,000
- **Needle Cricothyrotomy** (last resort): Only for complete obstruction unable to clear; age ≥ 3, weight ≥ 15 kg, unconscious

**R-3: Acute Respiratory Distress**
- High-flow O₂, CPAP for moderate-severe, limit exertion
- **Asthma/COPD Severe:** Continuous Albuterol 5 mg via nebulizer, Epinephrine 1:1,000 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg) — use caution if > 35 yrs or CAD/HTN
- **CHF/Pulmonary Edema:** NTG by blood pressure (SBP > 200: 1.2 mg; 150-200: 0.8 mg; 100-150: 0.4 mg SL) every 5 min, IV fluid bolus (carefully)

**M-1: Allergic Reaction / Anaphylaxis**
- **Allergic Reaction (no anaphylaxis):** Diphenhydramine 50 mg IV/IM
- **Anaphylaxis (stridor, wheezing, airway edema, hypotension):**
  1. Epinephrine 1:1,000 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg)
  2. IV/IO bolus up to 2000 mL NS
  3. Diphenhydramine 50 mg IV
  4. Albuterol 5 mg if bronchospasm
  5. Dopamine 10 µg/kg/min if still hypotensive

**M-5: Overdose / Ingestion**
- Identify substance, bring container, check blood glucose
- **Narcotics (Respiratory Depression):** Naloxone 2 mg slow IV (may repeat × 2 every 2-3 min)
- **Hypoglycemia (Glucose ≤ 60):** Dextrose 50% 50 mL IV/IO (if no IV: Glucagon 1 mg IM/IN)
- **Tricyclic Antidepressants:** Sodium Bicarbonate 1 mEq/kg IV if SBP < 90, QRS > 0.12 sec, or seizures
- **Beta Blockers:** Atropine 1 mg → Glucagon 1 mg → Epinephrine 1:10,000 0.1 mg slow IV if SBP < 70
- **Organophosphate (Pesticide):** Atropine 2 mg IV every 3 min (NO MAX DOSE if bradycardic)

### When to Contact Base Hospital
- Any uncertainty about protocol adherence
- Deviations from standing orders
- Patient refusal of treatment
- Inability to establish IV after 2 attempts (ALS)
- Severe allergic reactions
- Respiratory compromise with failed airway attempts
- Cardiac dysrhythmias not responding to initial therapy

## 🛠️ How to Use the RAM Field Ops App

### Before Your Shift
1. **Check the Schedule** → confirm your unit assignment
2. **Check Unit Status** → ensure your unit is "In Service"
3. **Review Incident** → if assigned to an active incident, read incident details (location, contacts, resources)
4. **Equipment Check** → verify CS counts, vehicle documents, supplies

### During Patient Care
1. **Create Encounter**
   - If BLS-only: Simple Encounter (vital signs, chief complaint, disposition)
   - If ALS: Detailed Encounter (full PCR with history, exam, treatments)
2. **Enter Vitals** → heart rate, BP, SpO₂, temperature, GCS, pain scale
3. **Document Treatment** → medications given, IV access, airway management
4. **Enter Disposition** → transported to hospital, released at scene, AMA, etc.
5. **Record MAR** → if medications administered, enter dose/route/time/response

### Post-Care Documentation
1. **Progress Notes** → clinical assessment/plan (signed or unsigned)
2. **Sign Documents** → unlock encounter if PIN required
3. **Link to Incident** → ensure encounter auto-links to active incident
4. **Controlled Substance Entry** → log any CS given (paramedics)

### Troubleshooting
- **App crashed?** Restart app, your draft should auto-recover
- **Can't find an encounter?** Check filters (date range, unit filter, incident filter)
- **Questions about a field?** Ask Codsworth in the Chat
- **Think you found a bug?** Report it in the Chat with steps to reproduce

## ❓ Common Questions

**Q: I'm injured. What do I do?**
A: Report to your supervisor immediately. Seek medical care if needed. File an incident report with HR. Notify your emergency contact. An OSHA 301 form may be required.

**Q: Can I request time off?**
A: Submit requests 30 days in advance if possible. Urgent requests should go to Zach. Approved time off will be covered by schedule adjustments.

**Q: How do I get my credentials filed?**
A: Email scans/photos to your HR contact. Codsworth will auto-file them to your Drive folder and update your status in the employee tracker.

**Q: What if I disagree with a protocol?**
A: Follow the protocol in the field. Discuss concerns with a physician afterward. Protocols are based on SSV EMS Agency standards and physician consensus.

**Q: How do I report a safety concern?**
A: Tell your crew lead or Zach immediately. If it's urgent (equipment failure, patient safety), call Aaron. All reports are confidential.

**Q: What's the difference between a "Demob" and a "Unit Transfer"?**
A: **Demob** = unit is released from the incident entirely. **Transfer** = unit moves to a different incident. Data (encounters, MAR, supply runs) stays with the original incident either way.
